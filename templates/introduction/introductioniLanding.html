MORE ABOUT OUR WORK

Process developed for obtaining the information to be published

Our process is based on several stages. First, information is obtained from various related and prestigious sources that publish their scientific data on the internet. This information is structured and stored, and subsequently, cross-referencing techniques, expert criteria, and other methods are applied to guarantee its quality and veracity.

Once a sufficiently broad and reliable knowledge base is available, this information is processed to extract other data of extreme importance, such as SNPs, Allele Annotation, Allele frequency, conformation, ancestral data, age, location, color, parentage, recombination, three-dimensional backbone, etc.

This information allows us to perform 3D plotting of genes, as well as to map ancient human migrations and how they contributed to the genomic reservoir of current society. Furthermore, based on complex algorithms, we are able to evaluate the impact of diseases and medications on individuals, according to genomic studies performed.

Pharmacodynamic

Pharmacodynamics refers to the study of how drugs affect the body. It examines the mechanisms of drug action, including the relationship between drug concentration and effect, receptor interactions, and the physiological and biochemical responses produced. Understanding pharmacodynamics helps predict the efficacy and potential side effects of medications.

Pharmacokinetic

Pharmacokinetics is the study of how the body absorbs, distributes, metabolizes, and excretes drugs. It focuses on the movement of drugs within the body over time, including processes such as absorption, bioavailability, distribution, metabolism, and elimination. Pharmacokinetics is essential for determining appropriate drug dosages and schedules.

Genomic Diseases

Genomic diseases are conditions caused by alterations in an organism's DNA, ranging from single nucleotide changes to large chromosomal rearrangements.

These genomic variations can disrupt the normal function of genes, leading to a wide spectrum of health problems, including inherited disorders like cystic fibrosis or Huntington's disease, as well as complex conditions such as certain cancers and heart diseases. Understanding the specific genomic changes is crucial for diagnosis, prognosis, and the development of targeted therapies.

A New Perspective for Studying Their Origins

The understanding of genomics has advanced significantly with sequencing tools and data analysis. However, the inherent complexity of genomic interactions and the spatial organization of the genome are often lost in two-dimensional representations. Three-dimensional (3D) visualization of genes and their alleles emerges as a powerful tool that offers a revolutionary perspective, especially for unraveling the origins and evolution of these fundamental units of heredity.

Human Migration & Genomic Evolution

How ancient migrations shaped the genomic diversity of modern humanity

70,000 Years Ago

Out of Africa migration triggered major genomic bottlenecks and adaptive changes

Skin Pigmentation

Populations adapted to UV exposure with genomic variants in MC1R and SLC24A5 genes

Lactase Persistence

Arose independently in pastoral populations via MCM6 gene mutations within last 10,000 years

Cold Adaptation

Arctic populations developed variants in FADS and CPT1A genes for efficient lipid metabolism

Neanderthal Introgression

1-4% of non-African genomes contain Neanderthal DNA affecting immunity and metabolism

Founder Effects

Island and isolated populations show unique allele frequencies from small founding groups

Gene Flow Patterns

Trade routes and population movements created clines of genomicvariation across continents

Recent Selection

Agricultural transition drove rapid evolution in disease resistance and dietary genes

Have a question? Check out the FAQ

Learn about allele conformations: their types, how we visualize them, and how to interpret conformational summaries in the viewer.

What is genomic ancestry and how is it determined?

Genomic ancestry is inferred by comparing DNA segments to reference populations. Algorithms examine allele patterns and haplotypes to estimate ancestral contributions across the genome.

How do alleles influence trait inheritance?

Alleles are variant forms of a gene; combinations of alleles (homozygous/heterozygous) determine how traits are expressed. Some alleles are dominant, others recessive or additive.

How are allele conformations interpreted?

Conformations summarize allele structure and functional predictions. We show frequency across populations, predicted effects, and supporting evidence so researchers can interpret biological relevance.

What are the main types of allele conformation?

Conformation types typically include sequence-based classifications (allele sequence variants), structural conformations (insertions/deletions and rearrangements), and functional categories (predicted effect on gene product). Our viewer shows these aspects together to help evaluate biological relevance.
